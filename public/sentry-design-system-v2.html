§ 01

Philosophy

The analyst UI is for Sibe's team. The customer portal is for VDA's customers. They share a brand, a color palette, and a moral stance — but not a font, not a density, not a posture. Forcing them to look identical would break both.

This system exists because Halo failed for a reason we can name. It was built against a vendor pitch deck, not against a SOC analyst's daily workflow. Sibe's team routed around it within weeks. Jim absorbed 100+ hours configuring it alone. It expanded into CRM, project management, and invoicing before it could deliver the one thing it was asked to do — ticketing.

Sentry is the anti-Halo. Ticketing only. Narrower scope, shorter timeline, deeper discipline. The design system exists to hold that discipline in place when scope tries to creep.

Two surfaces, two dialects of the same brand. The analyst UI speaks in mono-spaced precision — it's a tool for people who triage twenty tickets before lunch. The customer portal speaks in plain-English calm — it's the surface customers log into to open a case or check status. Same brand orange. Same severity semantics underneath. Different voices, because they're serving different moments.

Sibe is in the tool from day one. Every screen is shaped against his daily workflow. That's the single most important rule in this document.
Rule 01
Design backward from Sibe's daily flow
The MVP is successful if Sibe's copy-paste count goes to zero. Every interaction the analyst UI ships must survive the "does Sibe do this twenty times a day" test. If the answer is yes, that interaction gets optimized ruthlessly.
Rule 02
Analyst-initiated, always
Not every Sniper alert becomes a ticket. The analyst decides. The UI respects that: no auto-escalation, no auto-ticket-creation from thin signals. The analyst is the intelligence layer. We build tools for them, not around them.
Rule 03
Wrong-client leakage is a UX bug
Jim flagged wrong-customer data leakage twice on the call. The compose flow prevents it structurally — customer context is pulled from the ticket, never from a free-text field. If a design decision lets an analyst type customer B's data into customer A's email, that design is wrong.
Rule 04
Security telemetry stays in Sniper
VDA sells the promise: "we don't store your data." The ticket references an incident ID back to Sniper. It isn't a second system of record for security events. Every screen should make the "just a reference, not a copy" relationship visually obvious.
Rule 05
Scope discipline is a design choice
Every component in this system exists to serve the 5-week ticketing MVP. When someone asks for a dashboard, a report, a chart, a knowledge-base — the answer is "Phase 2, separate doc." The design system itself is a scope-enforcement tool.
Rule 06
Ship in 5 weeks or stop
Every component here should be buildable with junior-level React skill. No exotic libraries, no complex state machines, no visual tricks that take a week to debug. If it can't ship in 5 weeks, it doesn't belong in the MVP design system.
§ 02

Brand

Sentry is the product. VDA Labs is the company behind it. 3Nails is the technical partner. The co-brand lives on every surface where it matters — the deck, the analyst UI header, the customer portal footer, the print exports.

Wordmark
SENTRY
Product name appears in Consolas, uppercase, with 0.2em letter-spacing. Always paired with the VDA orange. Never used decoratively — only as a proper wordmark in the header or footer.
Co-brand
VDA LABS × 3NAILS INFOSEC
The co-brand lockup appears in the deck header and in any VDA-facing document produced by 3Nails. Customer-facing surfaces (the portal, customer emails) show only VDA — the 3Nails partnership is visible internally, not externally.
Voice mark
Built for Sibe
The project has an internal identity — it's built for one primary user, named, visible, on Slack. That identity is not in the UI, but it's in every design review: "would Sibe understand this in two seconds?"
§ 03

Color

Deep navy. Steel blue. Burnt orange. The palette is pulled from the v4 SecOps deck. Cold enough to feel like an operator tool, warm enough in the accent to not feel sterile. Pure blacks and pure whites are banned. Burnt orange is reserved for emphasis — if a screen has more than one orange element, we've lost discipline.

The orange rule
Burnt orange is the product's fingerprint. Every screen should have exactly one orange moment — the selected ticket, the primary action button, the active SLA warning. More than one, and the eye stops knowing where to go.

§ 04

Typography

Three families. Three roles. Georgia for headlines and moments. Calibri for everything the user actually reads. Consolas for ticket IDs, timestamps, and anything that needs to line up. The type stack is pulled directly from the v4 deck so every artifact — deck, app, portal, email — feels like the same thing.

The one-sentence type rule
Does this text need to line up in a column? Consolas. Is it a headline, a ticket subject, or a quote? Georgia. Everything else? Calibri.
DO

Use Consolas for ticket IDs (VDA-1847), timestamps, customer IDs, and anything where horizontal alignment matters. The mono is a structural choice, not a decorative one.

DON'T

Don't use Consolas for body prose. Mono body kills readability at scale. If you're tempted to use it because "it feels technical," use Calibri with a mono eyebrow label instead.

Wrong-client prevention (compose flow)

The single most important UI pattern in the analyst compose flow. When an analyst composes an outbound customer email, the customer context fields are locked and prefilled from the ticket. They are not free-text. They cannot be edited. This is the structural prevention of the wrong-client-data leak Jim flagged on the call.

Compose reply · VDA-1847
Customer
Acme Corp (acme-corp)
LOCKED · FROM TICKET
To
security-contact@acme.com
LOCKED · FROM CUSTOMER RECORD
Subject
[VDA-1847] Unusual login activity on your CFO's account
LOCKED · FROM TICKET
The only editable field in the compose flow is the message body. Customer, recipient, and ticket reference are bound to the ticket record and cannot drift.
When
Every outbound customer email
No exceptions. Even "quick note to a customer" goes through the same compose flow. If an analyst needs to reach a customer, they do it from an open ticket.
Why
Historical baseline: multiple incidents per quarter
Jim named this twice on the discovery call. It's a real, recurring bug in the current email-based workflow. The fix is structural — there's no version of this UI where an analyst can free-type another customer's name into the To field.
Kill switch
Week 5 target: zero incidents
If wrong-customer leakage happens even once during the pilot, we stop and diagnose the compose flow before continuing. This is non-negotiable.
§ 06

Voice

The analyst UI names the ticket. The customer portal names the consequence. Same event. Same ticket. Different sentence. The analyst doesn't need hand-holding; the customer doesn't need jargon.

Event Analyst UI says Customer Portal says
New ticket from Sniper VDA-1847 · CRIT · acme-corp · 14m · sniper-incident=inc-4421 We've opened a case about unusual login activity on your account.
Email from customer threaded VDA-1847 · inbound · from:security@acme · matched via [VDA-1847] We received your reply — an analyst will respond shortly.
SLA approaching breach VDA-1847 · SLA 11m · SEV-CRIT · assignee: sibe.klomp We're actively working on your case. Next update within the hour.
Ticket resolved VDA-1847 · resolved · MTTR 42m · customer-confirmed Your case is resolved. A summary is available in your ticket history.
Customer hasn't replied VDA-1847 · awaiting-customer 18h · auto-reminder sent 2h ago Friendly reminder — we need your confirmation to close this case.
Analyst voice
Operator shorthand
Direct. Structured. Built for pattern-matching at speed. Ticket IDs, timestamps, customer slugs, SLA countdowns — all in mono, all aligned. Imperative verbs on buttons: REPLY, ASSIGN, RESOLVE, ESCALATE.
Customer voice
Plain-English CISO
Calm. Past tense for completed actions. Present tense for work in progress. Names the consequence before the mechanism. No ticket IDs in the body (they appear in the subject and metadata). No jargon that requires translation.
Template voice
Fill-in-the-blank, never fill-in-the-customer
Email templates have exactly two kinds of variables: {{ticket.*}} fields (automatic from the ticket record) and {{message_body}} (the analyst's free text). Customer name and contact come from the ticket, never from the analyst's typing.
Shared rules
Both voices agree
No emoji. No exclamation points. No "Great news!" No sycophancy. Numbers are exact or they aren't stated. If something went wrong, we say so directly — and name what we're doing about it.
DO — Customer portal

"We're investigating unusual login activity on your CFO's account. We'll update you within the hour."

DON'T — Customer portal

"Great news! Our AI-powered platform has detected a potential security event and our 24/7 SOC team is leveraging advanced detection capabilities to triage..."

DO — Analyst UI status

VDA-1847 · awaiting-customer 18h · auto-reminder sent 2h ago

DON'T — Analyst UI status

"Waiting for customer response... The system will automatically remind them."
