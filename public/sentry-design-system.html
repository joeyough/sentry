§ 01 · Philosophy

The analyst UI is for Sibe's team. The customer portal is for VDA's customers.

They share a brand, a color palette, and a moral stance — but not a font, not a density, not a posture.

This system exists because Halo failed for a reason we can name. It was built against a vendor pitch deck, not against a SOC analyst's daily workflow. Sibe's team routed around it within weeks. Jim absorbed 100+ hours configuring it alone. It expanded into CRM, project management, and invoicing before it could deliver the one thing it was asked to do — ticketing.

Sentry is the anti-Halo. Ticketing only. Narrower scope, shorter timeline, deeper discipline. The design system exists to hold that discipline in place when scope tries to creep.

Two surfaces, two dialects of the same brand. The analyst UI speaks in mono-spaced precision — it's a tool for people who triage twenty tickets before lunch. The customer portal speaks in plain-English calm — it's the surface customers log into to open a case or check status. Same brand orange. Same severity semantics underneath. Different voices, because they're serving different moments.

Sibe is in the tool from day one. Every screen is shaped against his daily workflow. That's the single most important rule in this document.

§ 02 · Rules

Seven rules that govern every decision.

  • 01
    Design backward from Sibe's daily workflow. Every screen must survive the "Sibe triages 20 tickets in 90 minutes on a Monday morning" test. If it slows that loop, cut it.
  • 02
    Wrong-client prevention on every outbound surface. Jim named this twice on the call. Cross-customer data leakage is the single most embarrassing failure mode a SOC can have. Compose flow shows customer name three times before send.
  • 03
    Ticketing only. Nothing else. No CRM. No project management. No invoicing. The things Halo tried to do that weren't the job.
  • 04
    Mobile is a peer, not a fallback. Sibe will triage on his phone during off-hours. The portal's customers will open cases from the airport. Both surfaces must work on a 375px viewport without compromise.
  • 05
    Severity is the ordering principle. Nothing else. CRIT first. Then HIGH. Then MEDIUM. Then LOW. Then resolved-at-bottom. No ranking machinery, no learning, no feedback loops. Just the order Sibe already uses in his head.
  • 06
    The ticket holds the communication log. SNYPR holds the telemetry. VDA's customer promise is "we don't store your data." That promise is protected by keeping Sentry as a communication-of-record system, not a data lake.
  • 07
    Every feature has a stakeholder quote behind it. If it's in the product and it's not traceable to a line Jim, Sibe, or Kendall said on the discovery call, it's in the scope audit doc as AT-RISK. No anticipatory scope.
§ 03 · Brand Colors

One orange. One navy. Everything else is a gradient between them.

The brand orange is used as an accent — for emphasis, severity, and a single focal point per screen. Never as decoration. Never as filler. If an orange accent appears on a screen, it is drawing the eye to the one thing that matters most.

Text runs on a three-tone scale, all solid hex: #E8EEF4 for primary text, #A8B8C8 for secondary, #6B7D91 for muted detail like timestamps. Surfaces step up from the navy page through #122238 cards, #1A2E47 edges, and #172941 raised fields, with #2A3E57 for dividers and hairlines. Steel blue #6B93B8 carries quiet UI accents and medium severity. One rule earned the hard way: no rgba on text. Translucent text plus synthesized font weights produced the "ghost text" that plagued earlier versions — solid hex values and weights 400 and 700 fixed it.

§ 04 · Typography

Georgia, Calibri, Consolas.

The app runtime uses three typefaces: Georgia for display, Calibri for body, Consolas for mono. Weights are 400 and 700 only.

This document is the one exception. It renders in the system font stack — SF on iOS, Segoe UI on Windows, Roboto on Android. Earlier versions used Georgia for display, and on iOS Safari, Georgia at synthesized weights rendered as translucent ghost text on dark backgrounds. The app runtime never hits that bug: it uses Georgia at 400 and 700 only, which are real glyphs on every platform. This standalone doc's layout did hit it, so the doc drops to system fonts. The scale and weights in the samples below are the app's — in the app, they render in Georgia, Calibri, and Consolas.

Rules

Weights are 400 or 700. No intermediate weights — they synthesize badly on iOS Safari. Line height is 1.5 for body, 1.2 for display. Letter-spacing is 0 for body, negative for display, positive for mono and eyebrows. Font-synthesis is explicitly disabled at the root.

§ 05 · Severity

Four levels. Same semantics on both surfaces. Different language.

Sibe's team thinks in CRIT / HIGH / MED / LOW — mono-spaced, scannable in a dense queue. Customers read in full words, with an SLA commitment attached so they know what to expect. Same underlying severity model; different dialect per surface.

CRIT

Response within 1 hour
HIGH
High
Response within 4 hours

Medium

Resolved tickets sink to the bottom of every queue regardless of severity. SLA clocks stop at resolution. No severity badge is bigger than 13px on any surface — the point of a severity label is recognition, not decoration.

§ 06 · Two Surfaces

Analyst console and customer portal.

The same brand, two dialects. The choices that differ between them are deliberate — each surface is tuned to the moment its user is in.

Analyst Console

Dense, fast, mono-accented.

Density: High. Sibe triages 20 tickets before lunch.

Mono usage: Ticket IDs, severity labels, SNYPR incident numbers.

Shortcuts: Keyboard-first on desktop, swipe-first on mobile.

Background: Navy. Dark mode is the default posture for a SOC tool.

Customer Portal

Calm, spacious, plain-spoken.

Density: Low. Customers visit occasionally.

Mono usage: Only where technical precision is required (ticket ID, timestamps).

Shortcuts: Tap targets and clear visual hierarchy.

Background: Light. Trust and clarity over SOC aesthetic.

§ 07 · Components

What every screen is built from.

Sentry has a small component set by design. Five things repeat across every surface. When a new pattern shows up, the first question is: can it be expressed as a variant of one of these?

Ticket row

The repeated unit of the analyst queue. ID, severity, subject, customer, status, SLA, owner. Mobile collapses to a stacked card with swipe-to-assign and swipe-to-resolve gestures.

Ticket card (portal)

The customer-facing equivalent. Same severity, fewer columns, gentler language. Tap opens detail view; no swipe destructive actions.

Compose flow

Always shows customer name three times before send. Wrong-client prevention is the most important UX in the app.

Status pill

Open / Claimed / Awaiting customer / Completed. Colored background at 12% alpha, solid colored text. Same palette on both surfaces.

SLA pill

Three states: on track, warning, breach. Warning at 75% of SLA remaining. Breach on exceed. Mono font so the countdown scans fast.

§ 08 · Voice

Plain, specific, earned.

Banned words: innovation, synergy, unlock, journey, transformation, best-in-class, leverage as a verb.

These are vendor words. Sentry is not a vendor — it's a tool built with Sibe, for Sibe's team.

When copy has a choice between a specific noun and a generic one, pick specific. "Cases" not "incidents." "Tickets" not "records." "Reply" not "communication." The software is called by the thing it does.

Error messages name the problem and the next step. Toast confirmations are one line. Empty states don't apologize. The voice across both surfaces is confident without being cute.

§ 09 · Mobile

Mobile is the first render, not the second.

Every layout decision starts at 375px. Desktop is an expansion of the mobile layout, not the other way around. Two reasons: Sibe will triage on his phone during off-hours, and customers will open cases from wherever they are.

Tap targets are 44px minimum. Swipe gestures are committed at a 120px threshold with snap-back below. Vertical scroll always wins over horizontal swipe — swiping a ticket row never hijacks the page scroll.

If it doesn't work with one thumb on a subway, it doesn't ship.

§ 10 · Changelog

How this document got here.

v1 was the pre-discovery sketch. Speculative. Killed on first contact with the discovery call.

v2 was what Vincent saw — rich dashboards, an alerts surface, experimental ranking rules, and a self-extension workbench for rule-based workflows. Kendall killed the ranking machinery after the discovery call clarified Jim's actual ask: ticketing, nothing else.

This document is the current system. Ticketing only. Two surfaces — analyst console and customer portal. One orange, one navy. Seven rules. Every feature traceable to a stakeholder quote.

Phase 1 ships in eight weeks: ticket queue, ticket detail, compose with wrong-client prevention, SNYPR bridge, inbound email bridge (soc@vdalabs.com), customer portal with case-open and ticket-view. Everything else is Phase 2 or out of scope. The copy-paste workflow Sibe's team does today — copy the customer email into SNYPR, copy the SNYPR incident back into an outbound email — goes away by week 5.

Rebuild note. This document was rebuilt after a rendering bug on iOS Safari made earlier versions look translucent on mobile. The cause was three things together: sticky nav with backdrop-filter, Georgia display type at synthesized weights, and rgba text colors. This rebuild eliminates all three: no sticky elements, system fonts at weights 400 and 700, and solid hex text colors with no rgba.
