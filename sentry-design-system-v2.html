§ 01

Philosophy

The console is for analysts. The client app is for owners. They share a brand, a color palette, a moral stance — but not a font, not a density, not a tone. Forcing them to look identical would break both.

Every SOC product on the market makes one of two mistakes. Either the analyst tool leaks into the client view — dense, jargon-heavy, full of MITRE IDs no business owner should have to read — or the client app's softness leaks back into the console and analysts lose trust in a toy.

Sentry solves this by treating the two surfaces as dialects of a shared language. The console speaks in mono-spaced precision. The client app speaks in serif calm. Both share the same vocabulary underneath: the VDA orange is the VDA orange. A critical severity is still a critical severity. The phishing attempt the analyst calls "T1566.001 × Spearphishing" is the same event the client sees as "we blocked 5 phishing emails targeting your finance team." Same fact. Different register.

The feedback loop is visible on both sides. When the client approves a blocked session, that approval feeds the scoring engine — and the client knows it. When they tell Sentry an asset is more critical than we thought, the next alert on that asset carries the weight. The client isn't a passive recipient; they're the teaching signal. Making that visible is how we earn trust faster than an MSSP that talks at them.

Analysts live in the console 8 hours a day. Owners open the client app for 90 seconds in an Uber. Designing for both at once means designing for neither well.
Rule 01
Shared brand, not shared UI
Colors, logo, and voice-intent travel across both apps. Type, density, and interaction models don't. If a decision makes the console feel like the client app (or vice versa), we've made the wrong decision.
Rule 02
Same fact, different register
Every piece of information the client sees also exists in the console — just reframed. The console names the MITRE technique; the client names the business consequence.
Rule 03
Calm earns attention
The client app should never feel noisy. When everything screams, the one critical alert gets ignored. Red is reserved for the 1% of moments that actually need it.
Rule 04
Density earns trust
The console should feel information-dense. Analysts who trust a tool are those who feel it shows them more than they could see on their own. Whitespace where a data point could go reads as amateur.
Rule 05 · New
Show what we've already done
Before asking the client to decide anything, the app tells them what VDA has already done about it. Detection → isolation → notification → analysis — all visible before the decision buttons appear. Trust is earned through the timeline, not the outcome.
Rule 06 · New
Teaching is a two-way loop
When the client teaches Sentry something — an asset is more critical, an alert was unclear — the system acknowledges immediately and shows the teaching will carry forward. "Noted · carried forward" is not decoration; it's the product.
§ 02

Brand

Sentry is the product. VDA Labs is the company behind it. Both live on every screen. The orange is the throughline — it's the fingerprint that appears in both apps, in the print report, in the board brief, in the email signature.

Wordmark
SENTRY · CLIENT
The product name SENTRY always appears in JetBrains Mono, uppercase, with 0.25em letter-spacing. In the console it's tight against the shield icon. In the client app it appears as SENTRY · CLIENT above the client's own name.
Icon mark
The shield in a ring
A Lucide Shield glyph inside a 1px bordered square, with a tint of the brand orange. Size varies: 30px in the console header, 72px in the client app's status card, 48px in print.
Co-branding
VDA × 3Nails Infosec
On client-facing materials (board brief, email digest, welcome flow), the VDA Labs logo appears alongside 3Nails Infosec as the technical partner. In the console and client app, only VDA appears.

§ 04

Typography

Three type families, four roles. Mono for instruments. Display serif for moments. Print serif for paper. Sans for body prose. Each carries a different emotional weight — they're the single biggest lever we use to make the console feel like an instrument, the client app feel like a confidant, and the board report feel like a document worth archiving.

Sans · The prose voice

The sentence that decides which font
Is this an analyst looking at structured data? → JetBrains Mono. Is this the hero of a client screen? → Fraunces. Is this the hero of a printed page? → Cormorant Garamond. Is this more than two sentences of prose? → Inter.
DO

Use Cormorant Garamond for board-report titles and metric numbers. It's the serif that earns a place on a CFO's desk — denser vertical rhythm than Fraunces, better at small sizes in print, unmistakably archival.

DON'T

Don't mix Fraunces and Cormorant in the same surface. The client app is a Fraunces world. The board report is a Cormorant world. A modal that bridges both (say, an export preview) picks the destination's typeface, not the origin's.

§ 06

Patterns

Five named patterns that carry trust. These are the compositions — not primitives — that earn their own page because they recur across screens, contexts, and versions. When a new feature ships, it reaches for these first.

Response Timeline

The "what we've done" pattern. Every decision surface should show the client — in chronological order — what VDA has already handled before asking them to act. Sage-green accent. Pulsing dot on the current step. Named analyst on each completed action.

Live example · decision screen
What we've done
SLA 12 MIN
  • Suspicious login detected
    14 min ago
  • Session isolated by VDA
    12 min ago · T. REEVES
  • Threat intel cross-reference complete
    8 min ago · matched 3 related events
  • Awaiting your decision
    SLA: call Tom within 15 min of approval
When
Every decision screen, always
If the client is being asked to do anything, the timeline appears before the decision buttons. For informational items, timeline still appears — it just ends with "closed · recorded in digest" instead of a waiting state.
Rules
Steps are specific, named, timestamped
Each step includes what happened, when, and — where applicable — who did it. Generic steps like "analyzing" or "in progress" are banned. If a step is active work, it gets the pulsing current-dot treatment.
SLA badge
Top right, always visible
Shows either a live countdown (SLA 12 MIN) or an INFORMATIONAL label for items that don't require action. The dot pulses when an SLA clock is running.

Teach-Back Card

The bidirectional feedback loop. Any moment where the client can teach Sentry something about their environment — asset criticality, tuning preference, severity feeling — gets this treatment. Brand-orange accent, "Before you decide" eyebrow, three-option button row.

Live example · decision screen
Before you decide
How critical is this asset, really?
Your answer tunes how Sentry scores future alerts on Tom Webb. This is the loop that makes detection smarter for your environment specifically.
Placement
Between timeline and decision
Teach-back appears after the response timeline and before the decision buttons. This is deliberate: the client sees what we've done, teaches the system, then decides. Not after.
Eyebrow
"Before you decide"
The eyebrow label is always Before you decide in the orange brand color. The title is always a short question in serif. The body names the specific thing their answer affects.
Options
Always three, always honest
Teach-back presents three options: elevate, keep, lower. Two is coercive; four is analysis paralysis. The "keep" / "just right" option is as valid as the others — it teaches Sentry that the current scoring is calibrated.

Noted Pill

The micro-acknowledgment. When a client teaches Sentry something and no other action is needed, a solid-orange pill rises above the originating element, holds for 2 seconds, then fades and retreats. It's the UI equivalent of "heard you."

Anatomy
Noted · carried forward
When
Input accepted, no further action
Use for: teach-back selections, preference toggles, dismissed items. Don't use for: confirmed decisions (those get the full green toast), errors, or anything requiring follow-up.
Timing
2.4s · pop / hold / fade
0–200ms rise + overshoot. 200ms–1.9s hold. 1.9s–2.4s fade upward and dissolve. This timing feels alive without blocking. Never use fast-in-fast-out (feels like a glitch) or slow-fade (feels like a bug).
Copy
Short · specific · past-tense
"Noted · carried forward." "Saved to your preferences." "Muted for this week." Always names what happened, never asks for more input. Never uses exclamation points.

The Take Banner

The BCG / McKinsey move. The top of every executive summary document leads with a black-on-cream banner that states the week's conclusion in one sentence. The board reads this first; the data follows.

Live example · board report exec summary
The take
VDA surfaced 2 of 3,547 signals for executive review this week — a 96% noise reduction. The signal-to-board ratio is healthy; current escalation discipline is working.
Position
Above the fold, always
The banner appears at the very top of the executive summary page, before any number, chart, or headline. If the CFO only reads one thing, it's this. Never buried in a sidebar or below metrics.
Voice
Declarative · no hedging
One sentence. Names the number. States the conclusion. Uses verbs like surfaced, reduced, maintained — not potentially, may, appears to. If we're not confident enough to say it declaratively, we shouldn't include it.
Design
Black banner, gold label, cream body
Background #1A1A1A. Label in Cormorant gold (#C8A86B), mono, 0.3em tracking. Body in Cormorant Garamond, cream (#F5F3EE), 16px. This is the only place dark mode lives inside a light-mode document — that's the point.

SLA Badge

The commitment indicator. A small pill that shows either a countdown on an active response clock or INFORMATIONAL for items with no SLA. Appears in the header of timeline cards and on the home-screen attention summary.

Anatomy · two states
SLA 12 MIN
INFORMATIONAL
Active state
Live countdown, pulsing dot
Shows remaining time until commitment breach. Dot pulses at 2.5s intervals. Color matches status — sage green for on-track, status-attention red if within 25% of breach.
Informational state
Solid dot, no animation
Used for items that don't carry a response commitment — phishing blocked, patch applied, weekly digest items. Muted color, same pill shape so the UI feels consistent across both states.
§ 07

Voice

The console names the technique. The client names the consequence. Same event. Same truth. Different sentence.

Event Console says Client app says
Phishing blocked T1566.001 × 5 · sig.t1566_001.447 · QUARANTINED We blocked 5 phishing emails targeting your finance team.
Credential dump T1003.001 · LSASS memory access · ESCALATED · PB-002 Someone tried to steal passwords from your accounting server. We've locked it down.
Suspicious login T1078 · Valid Accounts · Lagos/NG · sev 82 Unusual login from a new country. Need your call.
Patch applied Scheduled maintenance · SVR-TRADE-07 · complete · 0 regressions Security patch applied to your trading server during maintenance.
Quiet week Noise-to-signal ratio 98.4% · 0 escalations · all baselines green A quiet week. Nothing reached you.
Console voice
Partner-level operator
Direct. Technical. No fluff. Assumes the reader knows MITRE ATT&CK and reads log lines for a living. Uses present tense and active voice. If there's a decision, the verb is imperative: ESCALATE, SUPPRESS, CONFIRM.
Client voice
CISO explaining to the CEO
Calm. Plain-English. Names the consequence before the mechanism. Uses first-name addressing where natural. Never uses MITRE IDs, log strings, or severity numbers. Always tells the client what VDA has already done and what (if anything) they need to do.
Timeline voice
Past-tense · named · timestamped
Every timeline step uses past-tense completed action (isolated, quarantined, applied) or present continuous waiting state (awaiting your decision). Never future tense. Every step that involved a human names the analyst. Every step has a timestamp.
Report voice
The Take · one sentence · declarative
The Take banner states the week's conclusion as a single declarative sentence with a specific number. If the week was quiet, say it was quiet. If the volume spiked, say it spiked. Never say "some things happened."
Shared rules
Both voices agree
No emoji. No exclamation points. No "Great news!" openers. No sycophancy. No hedging when precision matters. Numbers are exact or they aren't stated. If something went wrong, we say so directly and explain what we're doing about it.
DO — Client app

"We caught 47 phishing emails aimed at your team this week. None reached anyone. We'll include the details in your weekly digest."

DON'T — Client app

"Great news! 🎉 Our AI-powered threat detection platform identified and neutralized 47 sophisticated spear-phishing attempts leveraging advanced social engineering tactics..."

DO — Timeline step

"Session isolated by VDA · 12 min ago · T. Reeves"

DON'T — Timeline step

"Taking action on the suspicious session · in progress..."
